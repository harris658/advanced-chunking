Indian ethnic menswear has roots stretching back thousands of years. The sherwani,
originally a long coat worn by Mughal nobility, evolved into a symbol of formal
Indian masculine dress. It typically reaches below the knees and is worn over a
kurta with churidar or straight-cut pants.

Bandhgala, also known as the Jodhpuri suit, combines Western tailoring with Indian
fabric traditions. It features a mandarin collar and is often made in rich fabrics
like silk, wool, or brocade. The style originated in Jodhpur and became popular
in royal courts during the British era.

Khadi is a hand-spun, hand-woven cloth central to Indian identity. It was
popularised by Mahatma Gandhi as a symbol of self-reliance. Today, khadi is
used in contemporary ethnic menswear for its breathable texture and artisanal
character.

Banarasi silk, woven in Varanasi, is one of India's finest textiles. Known for
its gold and silver brocade work, it has been used in royal garments for centuries.
Modern designers use Banarasi fabric in sherwanis and kurtas for weddings and
festive occasions.

Zari embroidery uses metallic threads—historically real gold and silver—woven
into fabric to create intricate patterns. It is a core technique in many luxury
ethnic garments, particularly in bridal and festive menswear.

Block printing is a traditional craft where carved wooden blocks are used to
stamp patterns onto fabric. Rajasthan and Gujarat are known centres for this
technique. Fabrics like cotton and linen with block print motifs are popular in
casual ethnic kurtas.

The kurta-pajama set is the most versatile piece in Indian ethnic menswear,
worn from casual daily use to semi-formal events. Fabric choice—cotton for
summer, wool or velvet for winter—determines the occasion. Embroidery, pintucks,
and mirror work can elevate a simple kurta to festive wear.
